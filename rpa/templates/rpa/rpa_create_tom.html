{% blocktranslate %} RPA Technical and organisational measures (TOM) {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} According to DPO experience, the TOM are the hardest part of an RPA. Please make sure to take your time to fill in the required information and be correct. State the measures you actually take (and not those you think the DPO wants to read). In case you cannot fill in the form at once, but want to save your progress, just enter "to be done" or something similar in the free-text fields of the area for action that needs your attention in the future in order to be able to save the form. You can then continue editing at another time. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Describe the technical and organisational measures (TOM) you take to properly handle the personal data in your responsibility. First, choose how to add TOM to your RPA: If your processing activity is examined and certified or in accordance with an existing data security concept, choose the appropriate option, leave all other fields blank and add the corresponding documents or references to these documents to your annex. You may also describe your TOM in a separate document and add it to the annex. The final option is to describe your TOM in the fields below. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} The GDPR on TOM (Art. 32): "Taking into account the state of the art, the costs of implementation and the nature, scope, context and purposes of processing as well as the risk of varying likelihood and severity for the rights and freedoms of natural persons, the controller [...] shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk, including inter alia as appropriate: [...]". This is followed by the first five areas for action as listed below. Not explicity listed but required ("inter alia") and recommended to be included in your RPA are the last three areas for action (appropriation, transparency and subject rights). {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} The areas for action in a nutshell: {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Pseudonymisation: Although pseudonymised data are considered personal data (in contrast to anonymised data), using pseudonymisation when processing data offers a higher level of protection. Check and/or describe the measures you take, such as the rule of pseudonymisation (number, hash, given by the data subject, etc.), regulation of reasons for decontrol of pseudonymisation, access to allocation lists and the documentation of the parts of a data set that undergo pseudonymisation (name, date of birth, etc.). If your processing activity does not allow pseudonymisation, check the corresponding option and leave everything else blank. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Encryption: Using encryption for data in transfer and at rest is an obvious way to protect data (and hence the rights of your data subjects). If you transfer personal data via a public network (e.g. using Email, VPN-access, Web-based applications, etc.), always use state-of-the-art end-to-end encryption such as TLS, S/MIME or GPG. Also consider using encrypted storage leveraging BitLocker, VeraCrypt, LUKS or similar state-of-the-art technology. If your processing includes special categories of personal data (Art. 9 GDPR) or if you use a mobile device for processing (higher risk for loss, damage or theft), encrypted storage is highly recommended or may even be mandatory. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Confidentiality/Integrity: You have to have the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality and integrity of processing systems and services. There are very many measures, that will be helpful in this regard, such as establishing a concept for data access based on balanced roles and access rights, having regularly revised security guidelines known and followed by all persons with access to data, having systems for data processing secure and up-to-date on the software side, secure erasure of data, just to name a few. Confidentiality and integrity mostly boils down to one important thing: Never ever lose control of the data in your responsibility. Never allow personal data to leak to unauthorized third parties or (even worse) the general public. Unfortunately, data leaking happens quite easily: Laptop lost, stolen or malware infected, wrong persons cc'ed in an Email, unintended or unauthorized upload to a cloud storage service, open data disposal containers left unattended in areas accessible to unauthorized persons, etc. etc. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Availability/Resilience: You have to have the ability to ensure the ongoing availability and resilience of processing systems and services. You also have to ensure the ability to restore the availability and access to personal data in a timely manner in the event of a physical or technical incident. This is mostly important for processing activites, where a failure is likely to entail negative consequences for data subjects. If your processing activity is a pure research project, availability and resilience are usually less important, as far as the rights of data subjects are concerned (since a failure may very well entail negative consequences for your research, though, you may want to put some thought into availability and resilience - you want to have at least two data backups proven to be restorable and on different physical locations). {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Evaluation: You have to establish a process for regularly testing, assessing and evaluating the effectiveness of technical and organisational measures for ensuring the security of the processing. This process can be either self-developed or already existing and approved for this purpose. Measures taken could include revision of concepts on a regular basis, frequent checks for recent software weaknesses (possibly automated) and penetration testing. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Appropriation: You are required to take measures to avoid the use of the data for other purposes than outlined in this RPA. That doesn't mean, that additional or changed purposes are impossible, but they require a formal and lawful procedure (that could be described or referenced below). Actual measures could include the use of pseudonymisation (as mentioned above), separation of the processing activity in terms of roles and organisational units and shutdown of interfaces of the systems used for processing that are not strictly required or limitations on those interfaces. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Transparency: Your processing activity must be transparent for all involved parties (data subjects, controllers and supervisory authorities). This requires measures such as documentation of data subject enquiries, the use of comprehensible language for answers to enquiries and extensions to databases or similar data structures to handle enquiries (lock flags, fields for notes on consent, objection, etc.). You should know or at least be able to make an educated guess about the number of your data subjects at all times, since this will be one of the first questions that you will be asked in case of an incident (you will be able to answer most other questions at the latest after having consulted your carefully prepared and maintained RPA). {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Subject rights: Data subjects have several rights against the data controller, including, but not limited to the right of access to personal data, the right of rectification and erasure (the latter is conditional and depends on several factors), the right to know about possible transfer to third parties, the right to know about automated decision making, the right to lodge a complaint with the supervisory authority (which will then, most likely, contact the controller and demand information about the processing), the right to know the source of the data (in case the data does not originate directly from the data subject). Possible measures include the implementation of standardized interfaces to collect all data of a single data subject (that could ideally be used by data subjects themselves), the use of software that follows the "data protection by design" and/or "data protection by default" principles and a design of the systems used for the processing activity, that allows to deactivate single features without affecting the processing as a whole. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} You probably noticed that there are intersections between the areas for action above. Describe a measure you take where you think it fits best and don't spend too much time thinking about this. The important part is to describe the measure and to act accordingly. {% endblocktranslate %}

{% blocktranslate %} Remember that measures shall be appropriate: If your processing activity is easy to comprehend, does not include special categories of personal data, is only done once with subsequent data erasure/anonymisation and affects only a manageable number of data subjects, some simple measures may already be sufficient. The farther your processing activity is away from this, the more measures (typically with an increasing level of effort and complexity) will be required. {% endblocktranslate %}
